## Email 1: Welcome (Day 0, immediate)

**Subject:** Welcome — here's what you just signed up for
**Preheader:** A quick note on what this is, what it isn't, and the one piece to read first.

---

Hey,

Welcome to Presence Under Pressure.

Before I tell you what this letter is about, let me tell you what it isn't.

It's not a tips-and-tricks newsletter. It's not "7 ways to speak with confidence." It's not communication advice that sounds good at a conference and dissolves the moment you're actually under pressure.

What it is: a weekly letter on the gap between capability and perception. Why capable people — founders, executives, genuinely talented humans — routinely fail to land what they actually know. What gets in the way. And what to do about it.

I write from a weird vantage point. IDF combat medic. Ordained rabbi. Several years as a community rabbi in London, which included roughly a hundred funerals — rooms full of grieving people where you have no choice but to find the thing that is both true and useful, and say it in a way that lands. You learn fast, doing that work, that the gap between knowing what to say and being able to say it when it counts is wider than almost anyone appreciates.

That's the thread. That's what we're here to pull on.

Most weeks run 400-600 words. Dense with insight, light on filler. I write for smart, busy people who are going to act on this — so I won't waste your time.

**To start:** if you read one thing from the archive, read this one — [**"If I could only tell you one thing about communication..."**](https://pathoslabs.co/writing/if-i-could-only-tell-you-one-thing-about-communication.html) It's the foundation everything else builds on.

One favour: hit reply and tell me what brought you here. One sentence is fine. It trains deliverability (I end up in your inbox instead of Promotions), and it helps me understand who I'm writing for.

Talk soon,

## Email 2: Best work (Day 3)

**Subject:** The piece most people forward
**Preheader:** Alex Hormozi, Tony Robbins, and why even $100M doesn't fix your nervous system.

---

Hey,

A few days in — figured I'd share the piece that gets forwarded more than anything else I've written.

It starts with a confession: I watched the Alex Hormozi / Tony Robbins interview and what jumped off the screen immediately wasn't the content. It was Hormozi's nervousness. The guy has built over $100M in businesses and he was visibly rattled in a podcast chair.

Most people's reaction to that is some version of "wow, even he gets nervous?" And that's true, but it's the wrong question.

The right question is *why* — and the answer has nothing to do with net worth.

[**Even $100M entrepreneurs get nervous (here's why)**](https://pathoslabs.co/writing/even-100m-entrepreneurs-get-nervous-heres-why.html) covers Keith Johnstone's status theory — the idea that every interaction has someone running higher status and someone running lower, and there's no neutral ground — and Lisa Feldman Barrett's work on constructed emotion, which basically says the story you tell yourself about your sensations is largely what *generates* the emotion.

That second part is the one most people haven't heard. And it changes what you do about nerves, permanently.

Worth the 5 minutes. Most people who read it tell me it reframed something they'd been misunderstanding for years.

Sam

## Email 3: The gap (Day 7)

**Subject:** The gap nobody talks about
**Preheader:** It's not about ability. It never is.

---

Hey,

Seven days in. Time to talk about the thing that's actually at the centre of all of this.

I've worked with founders raising series B rounds, CEOs addressing boards, senior executives trying to get a decision through a room that doesn't want to move. And not one of them — not once — had a capability problem.

Every single one knew what they were talking about. They were, by any reasonable measure, brilliant at their actual jobs.

The problem was the gap.

The gap is the space between what you know and what other people receive. Between who you are in a room of people who already trust you, and who you appear to be in a room that's still forming its opinion. It's the version of you that shows up when the stakes are real — and sometimes that version is significantly less impressive than the one you know yourself to be.

The gap isn't a knowledge problem. You don't close it by knowing more. You don't close it by taking another course, reading another book, or developing a thicker skin.

It closes when you can close it under pressure — not in theory, not in the mirror, not when you're calm and prepared and the room is friendly. When the investor is skeptical. When the board member is visibly checking their phone. When you've just been interrupted and you've lost the thread.

*That* is the real test. And most communication training never gets anywhere near it.

I wrote about the mission behind this work — where it came from, what I'm actually trying to build — in one of the earliest pieces. [**Your mission, should you choose to accept it.**](https://pathoslabs.co/writing/your-mission-should-you-choose-to-accept-it.html) It's a bit more personal than most of what I write. It came out of standing in a cold London hall at a funeral and deciding I didn't want to become the "almost man." Worth a read if you want to understand why I'm doing this.

The weekly letters are all variations on the gap — different angles, different case studies, different tools. That's what you'll get every week.

Sam
