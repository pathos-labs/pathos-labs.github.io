Your Pattern

Here's the thing nobody tells you about blind spots: you can't see them. That's the whole problem — and it's the reason this particular pattern is so hard to name, so hard to own, and so hard to change without outside help. You're not resisting feedback. You're not avoiding growth. You're standing in the one place from which the pattern is invisible.

Let me be direct about what that means. You walk out of a meeting thinking it went fine — maybe even well — and later discover that someone felt steamrolled. Or confused. Or dismissed. You give a presentation and your internal experience says "solid," but the room's experience was something different. The gap between how you think an interaction went and how others experienced it isn't occasional. It's consistent. And the consistency is what makes it a pattern rather than a bad day.

This isn't about intelligence. It's not about emotional range or caring about other people — you probably care quite a bit. It's about a specific cognitive capacity called metacognitive monitoring: the ability to observe your own communication while it's happening. Think of it as a feedback channel. Most communicators have an internal signal that reports, in rough real time, how they're landing. "They look confused — I should slow down." "That joke didn't land — pivot." "She's checked out — change something." For your profile, that channel is weak. Not broken — weak. The signal is there, but it's quiet, delayed, or distorted enough that you're consistently working with inaccurate data about your own impact.

Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham described this in 1955 with what they called the Johari Window — a model of self-knowledge divided into four quadrants. There's what you know about yourself and others know too (the open area). What you know but hide (the hidden area). What nobody knows (the unknown). And then the one that matters here: the blind spot. Things others can see about you that you genuinely cannot see about yourself. Everyone has a blind spot quadrant. For most people, it's small — a few quirks, a couple of habits they'd be surprised to hear about. For your profile, this quadrant is larger. And it's concentrated specifically in communication — how you come across, what impact you're having, what the room is actually experiencing while you're talking.

John Flavell's foundational work on metacognition in 1979 draws the distinction between cognitive ability and metacognitive ability — the capacity to think versus the capacity to monitor your own thinking. You can be exceptionally smart and have underdeveloped metacognitive monitoring. In fact, that combination is common in high-performers. You've succeeded on the strength of your ideas, your work ethic, your expertise. Nobody told you the self-observation channel needed separate development, because for a long time, the results were good enough that it didn't seem to matter.

Until it does. The cost of this pattern isn't dramatic — it's cumulative. The same feedback keeps coming back. "You talk over people." "You didn't read the room." "You lost them in the first five minutes." And each time, it's a surprise. Not because you're ignoring the feedback — you heard it last time, you tried to adjust — but because you can't see the pattern while it's happening. You're trying to fix something in the dark. You make the adjustment you think is right, and it's the wrong adjustment, or it's the right one applied at the wrong moment, and the feedback comes back again in a slightly different form.

Here's what I want you to hear clearly: you're not broken. The metacognitive monitoring channel is a skill, not a trait. Susan Ashford and Anne Tsui's research on feedback-seeking behaviour, originally published in 1991, demonstrated that people who actively seek external feedback perform better over time — not because they're more talented, but because they're building an external data system to supplement whatever internal monitoring they have. Your internal mirror is unreliable. The solution isn't to squint harder into it. The solution is to build better mirrors — external feedback loops, observation practices, and structured self-review protocols that give you accurate data about your own impact. That's what this playbook builds.

Your Scores Explained

Five constructs. Each one measured by a validated instrument. For the Blind Spot profile, the signature isn't one elevated score — it's the unreliability of the self-report itself. Here's what that looks like in practice.

Insight is your dominant construct, and it's low. This is measured by the CSAS — adapted from metacognitive awareness instruments for communication-specific self-monitoring. A low score here means limited visibility into your own communication patterns. Not limited intelligence, not limited self-awareness in other domains — specifically limited ability to observe how you're communicating while you're doing it. This is the core of the Blind Spot profile. Everything else follows from it.

Pressure is variable, and the variability itself is informative. Without reliable self-monitoring, it's genuinely difficult to know how pressure affects your communication. You might handle pressure well. You might handle it poorly. The measurement is impaired because the instrument doing the measuring — your own metacognitive awareness — is the thing that's underdeveloped. The PRCA-24 score tells you what you think pressure does to you. Your colleagues might tell you something different.

Anxiety is typically low for your profile, and that's worth paying attention to. The BFNE-II measures fear of negative evaluation — and many Blind Spot profiles don't have much of it. You're not afraid of what people think. But the absence of anxiety can actually be a contributor here. A moderate amount of social anxiety functions as a self-monitoring signal — "Am I landing? Are they with me?" — and without it, there's one fewer prompt to check the receiving end of your communication.

Skill Gap is variable — but here's the catch: you may not know whether it's variable up or down. The SPCC measures self-perceived communication competence, but for the Blind Spot profile, self-perception is exactly what's impaired. Your skills may be stronger than you think, or weaker than you think, or strong in some contexts and weak in others that you haven't noticed. The measurement instrument itself is unreliable.

Identity is similarly variable. The Clance Impostor Phenomenon Scale tells you about your self-concept — but when the mirror is distorted, the self-concept it reflects may not match reality. Some Blind Spot profiles have an inflated sense of their communication ability. Others have an unnecessarily diminished one. The point isn't which direction the distortion goes — it's that without external calibration, you can't know which version you have.

Your 4-Week Practice Plan

Eight exercises across four weeks. The logic is sequential: Week 1 collects external data you don't currently have. Week 2 builds the observation skills to use that data. Week 3 aligns your intended impact with your actual impact. Week 4 installs the permanent feedback infrastructure so you don't slide back. The order matters — you need the data before you can read it, and you need to read it before you can act on it.

Week 1 — External Data Collection

Exercise 1: External Feedback Blitz 5 conversations × 10 min

Your internal mirror is unreliable. So we start by building an external one. This week, you're going to ask five people — colleagues, direct reports, peers, anyone who's seen you communicate in professional contexts — one specific question. Not "How am I as a communicator?" That gets polite, vague, useless answers. The question is this:

  1. Choose 5 people who've seen you in meetings, presentations, or conversations. Mix levels — don't just ask people who report to you.
  2. Ask each one: "What's one thing I do in communication that I probably don't realize I do?"
  3. Listen. Don't explain. Don't defend. Don't contextualize. Just write down exactly what they say.
  4. Thank them. That's it for now. You're collecting data, not acting on it yet.

Ashford and Cummings demonstrated in 1983 that active feedback-seeking — specifically asking for information rather than passively monitoring — produces more accurate self-knowledge and better performance outcomes over time. The question is designed to bypass social politeness. "What don't I realize?" gives people permission to name the thing they've been noticing but haven't mentioned. Five people gives you enough data points to see patterns — if three of them say a version of the same thing, that's your blind spot.

You have five answers written down. At least two of them surprise you — genuinely surprise you, not "I kind of knew that." If nothing surprises you, either you asked the wrong people or you asked the question in a way that invited safe answers. Try again with different people and the exact phrasing above.

Exercise 2: Video Self-Review 30 min

Video bypasses your internal narrative about what happened. It shows you what actually happened. This is uncomfortable — almost everyone dislikes watching themselves — but for the Blind Spot profile, it's the single most efficient way to see what you've been missing.

  1. Record one meeting or presentation this week. Use Zoom's recording feature, a phone on a shelf, whatever's available. Get permission from participants if needed.
  2. Watch it twice. First pass: sound off. Watch only your body language, your energy, your pacing. Notice when you lean forward and when you retreat. Notice your facial expressions during other people's contributions. Notice what you do with your hands.
  3. Second pass: sound on. Listen to your clarity, your filler words, your message structure. Notice when you're concise and when you ramble. Notice when you check for understanding and when you barrel through.
  4. Write down three observations — things you see on video that contradict your memory of how the interaction felt from the inside.

The gap between experienced self and observed self is the blind spot made visible. Research on self-confrontation techniques — originally developed in counselling psychology by Kagan in 1975 — shows that video review produces faster gains in self-awareness than any other single intervention. Your memory of a conversation is filtered through your internal narrative. Video isn't. It's the closest thing to seeing yourself the way others see you.

You identified at least one thing on video that genuinely contradicts your memory of how it went. Maybe you thought you were being warm but you looked closed off. Maybe you thought you were listening but you were visibly waiting to talk. The contradiction is the data. If the video matches your memory perfectly, watch it again with someone else and ask what they notice.

Week 2 — Audience Observation + Pattern Recognition

Exercise 3: Audience Reaction Tracking 5 min after each of 3 meetings

You've been broadcasting without monitoring the receiving end. This exercise turns on the receiver. For the next week, you're going to watch your audience the way a stand-up comedian watches a room — not to perform, but to see what's landing and what isn't.

  1. Choose 3 meetings this week where you'll be speaking — they don't need to be high-stakes.
  2. During each meeting, track two things: when did someone disengage (eyes down, phone, posture shift, glazed look)? When did someone lean in (eye contact, nodding, forward posture, follow-up question)?
  3. For each shift you noticed, note what you were doing at that moment. Were you explaining something technical? Telling a story? Asking a question? Monologuing?
  4. Within 5 minutes of the meeting ending, write a quick note: "Engagement up when I ___. Engagement down when I ___."

Nonverbal decoding accuracy — the ability to read audience signals — is a trainable skill. Judith Hall's meta-analysis of interpersonal sensitivity research shows consistent improvement with deliberate practice. For the Blind Spot profile, the issue isn't that you can't read people in principle — it's that you haven't been practicing reading them during your own communication. Your attention has been on your message, not on its reception. This exercise redirects that attention.

You can link specific behaviours of yours to specific audience reactions. "When I went into detail on the methodology, two people checked out. When I gave the bottom-line number first, everyone looked up." These links are the raw material of communication self-awareness. You're building the feedback channel that's been quiet.

Exercise 4: Feedback Integration 20 min

You now have data from three sources: five people's feedback (Exercise 1), video evidence (Exercise 2), and your own audience observations (Exercise 3). Time to find the pattern.

  1. Lay out your Week 1 feedback answers. Read them together. What's the common thread?
  2. Compare with your video observations. Does the video confirm what people told you?
  3. Compare with your audience tracking notes. Do you see the same pattern showing up in real-time audience reactions?
  4. Name the pattern in one sentence. This is your blind spot — the specific thing everyone else can see that you couldn't. "I talk over people when I get excited." "I skip context because I assume people know what I know." "I don't check whether people are following before I move on." "I go monotone when I'm nervous and don't realize it."

Naming the pattern is the critical transition from "I have a vague sense that something's off" to "I have a specific, observable behaviour I can work on." Cognitive behavioural research consistently shows that specificity predicts change — a named target is a workable target. "I need to be a better communicator" is too vague to act on. "I don't check for understanding" is something you can fix starting tomorrow.

You have a named pattern that at least three of your five feedback sources confirmed, that you can see on video, and that you can connect to audience reactions. If only one source confirms it, you may have identified a real pattern — but keep collecting data. If no pattern emerges, the pattern might be subtler than one week of observation can catch, and that itself is useful information.

Week 3 — Impact Alignment

Exercise 5: Impact Intention Alignment 5 min before + 5 min after + 1 check-in

The gap between intended impact and actual impact is the blind spot made measurable. This exercise makes it concrete — three data points per interaction that show you exactly where the visibility breaks down.

  1. Before a meeting: Write one sentence — "The impact I intend to have is ___." Be specific. Not "I want it to go well." Something like: "I want them to leave feeling clear on the timeline and confident that we've thought it through."
  2. After the meeting: Write a second sentence — "The impact I think I had is ___." Don't filter this. Write your honest read of what just happened.
  3. Then ask someone who was in the room: "What impact did I have in that meeting? What's the one thing that landed — and the one thing that didn't?" Write down what they say, verbatim.
  4. Compare all three. The gap between your intended impact, your perceived impact, and the reported impact is the visibility gap. That gap is the data you've been missing.

This is a structured version of what Ashford and Tsui (1991) called "feedback-seeking for self-regulation" — using external data to calibrate internal models of performance. For the Blind Spot profile, the three-column comparison does something specific: it separates intention from self-perception from reality. Most people conflate these — "I wanted to be clear, therefore I was clear, therefore people experienced clarity." The exercise breaks that chain and shows you where the links actually are.

You can articulate the specific gap between your intended and actual impact. Maybe you intended confidence but came across as dismissive. Maybe you intended brevity but the room wanted more context. The specific nature of the gap tells you what to adjust — and more importantly, it tells you what your internal monitor is failing to report.

Exercise 6: Shadow Session 30 min

It's easier to see communication patterns in others than in yourself. This exercise builds the observation skill in a lower-stakes context — watching someone else — so you can then turn that skill inward.

  1. Sit in a meeting this week purely to observe. Not to contribute, not to evaluate — to study. Choose someone you consider an effective communicator.
  2. Watch for specific moves: When do they read the room and adjust? What signals do they pick up — a confused look, a disengaged posture, an energy shift — and what do they do in response? When do they pause? When do they check for understanding?
  3. Notice the micro-adjustments. Effective communicators are constantly calibrating — slowing down when someone looks lost, speeding up when the room is ahead of them, shifting register when the tone changes. These are the moves your metacognitive monitor is supposed to prompt.
  4. After the meeting, write down 2–3 specific moves you observed. Not "they were a good communicator" — specific behaviours. "When Sarah saw two people frown, she stopped and said 'Let me back up.' When she got a question, she repeated it before answering."

Observational learning — originally formalised by Albert Bandura in 1977 — is one of the most efficient pathways to skill acquisition. For the Blind Spot profile, the shadow session does double duty: it builds the observation skill itself (you're practising noticing communication patterns) and it gives you a reference library of specific moves you can adopt. You're essentially building the vocabulary your metacognitive monitor needs to operate.

You identified 2–3 specific communication moves that the person made — moves you wouldn't have noticed before you started this four-week process. If you're watching someone and thinking "they're just naturally good," look more carefully. Nobody is "naturally" good at reading a room. They're doing specific things. Your job is to see what those things are.

Week 4 — Ongoing Visibility Protocol

Exercise 7: Monthly Feedback Loop 15 min setup + 15 min/month ongoing

Self-awareness for the Blind Spot profile isn't a one-time fix. It's an ongoing channel. Your internal monitoring will improve with practice — it already has, if you've done the first three weeks — but it will never be fully self-correcting. The external feedback loop is the permanent replacement for the missing internal mirror.

  1. Choose 1–2 people you trust — someone who sees you communicate regularly and who will tell you the truth. Not the safest person. The most honest one.
  2. Have a direct conversation: "I'm working on my communication self-awareness. I want a monthly check-in with you — 15 minutes, same time each month. I'll ask you three questions: What are you seeing in my communication lately? What's improved? What's still showing up?"
  3. Schedule it. Put it in the calendar. Recurring. Monthly. The structure is the point — without it, the feedback stops when the initial motivation fades.
  4. At each check-in, compare what they report with your own self-assessment from that month. The gap between their report and yours is your remaining blind spot — and tracking that gap over time is how you measure progress.

Ashford and Cummings (1983) showed that active, structured feedback-seeking produces significantly better self-knowledge than passive monitoring alone. For the Blind Spot profile, this isn't optional — it's foundational infrastructure. Your internal mirror will always have some distortion. The monthly check-in is the external calibration that keeps the distortion from accumulating. Think of it like calibrating an instrument: the instrument works, but it drifts, and regular calibration keeps it accurate.

You have a scheduled monthly check-in with at least one person who has agreed to be direct with you. The first check-in has happened. You heard something you didn't expect — and you wrote it down instead of explaining it away. If your check-in partner only tells you things you already knew, either they're being too polite or your self-awareness has already improved significantly. Test it by asking: "What's the thing you're most reluctant to tell me?"

Exercise 8: Communication Journal 5 min per entry

The journal builds the metacognitive habit — the capacity to observe your own communication patterns that's been underdeveloped. It's simple, fast, and the cumulative effect is significant.

  1. After every significant interaction — any meeting, conversation, or presentation where the outcome mattered — write three lines:
    • What I intended: The impact I was trying to have.
    • What I think happened: My honest read of how it went.
    • What I'll check: The specific question I'll ask someone to verify my read.
  2. Keep it short. Three lines, not three paragraphs. The discipline is in the consistency, not the depth.
  3. Once a week, review the entries. Look for the gap between "what I think happened" and whatever external data you got. Is the gap narrowing? Is it consistent in one direction — do you consistently overestimate or underestimate your impact?
  4. After a month of entries, you'll have a dataset about your own communication patterns that you've never had before. The journal is the mirror you've been missing.

Flavell's metacognition research establishes that metacognitive skills — the ability to monitor and regulate your own cognitive processes — develop through deliberate practice, not passive experience. The three-line journal is a metacognitive exercise: it forces you to formulate an intention (planning), assess the outcome (monitoring), and identify a verification step (evaluation). Over time, these three operations become automatic — you'll start running them in real time during interactions, not just after them. That's the internal feedback channel coming online.

The journal is working when you notice yourself running the three-line assessment automatically — during the interaction, not just after it. When you catch yourself mid-meeting thinking "I intended X but I think I'm actually having Y impact, I should check" — that's the metacognitive channel activating. That's the blind spot shrinking. It won't disappear entirely. But it'll be small enough that the monthly feedback loop can handle the remainder.

Your Toolkit

Four protocols. These are the ongoing practices — not the four-week exercises, but the permanent infrastructure that keeps your blind spot visible. Print this. Revisit it monthly. These replace the internal feedback channel that isn't giving you accurate data.

Pre-Stakes — Impact Intention

Before any interaction where the outcome matters:

  1. Write one sentence: the specific impact I want to have in this interaction. Not "go well" — something observable. "I want them to leave clear on the three priorities." "I want her to feel heard on her concern."
  2. Name your blind spot pattern (from Exercise 4). Remind yourself: this is what I tend to miss.
  3. Set one observation target: "I'll watch for ___" — a specific audience signal you'll monitor during the interaction.

Sixty seconds. It won't feel like enough. It is — because the point isn't preparation, it's directing your attention to the receiving end of your communication before you start transmitting.

Real-Time — Room Scan

Every 5 minutes during a meeting or presentation:

  1. Eyes. Where are people looking? At you? At their laptops? At each other? Eyes tell you engagement levels faster than any other signal.
  2. Posture. Leaning in or leaning back? Open or closed? Arms crossed isn't always disengagement — but a room full of it probably is.
  3. Energy. Is the room's energy higher or lower than when you started? Are you raising it or draining it?
  4. The question is not "what do I assume?" The question is: what do I SEE? Report what you observe, not what you expect.

This will feel forced at first. Good. It should. You're manually running a process that other communicators run automatically. With practice, it'll speed up. But don't wait for it to feel natural — do it mechanically until the mechanism becomes habit.

Post-Event — Impact Gap Analysis

Within 1 hour of any significant interaction. Five minutes. Three columns:

  1. Intended impact — what you set out to do (from your pre-stakes intention).
  2. What I think happened — your honest internal read. Don't polish it. Write your actual impression.
  3. What someone else saw — ask one person from the room. Specific question: "What was your takeaway from that meeting?" or "How did my part land?"

The gap between columns two and three is the blind spot in that specific interaction. Track it over time. If the gap narrows, your self-monitoring is improving. If it stays wide, you need more external data — not more self-reflection.

Weekly — Feedback Request

One feedback request per week. Same structure every time:

  1. Choose someone who observed you communicate this week.
  2. Ask one specific question — not "how was I?" Something targeted: "Did I give enough context before the ask?" or "Was I talking over anyone in that discussion?" or "Did my energy match the room?"
  3. Write down the answer. Compare it with your own impression.
  4. Note the gap — or the absence of a gap. Both are data.

This is the maintenance protocol. The four-week plan builds the awareness. The weekly feedback request keeps it calibrated. Without it, the blind spot re-expands — not because you've failed, but because self-monitoring is a skill that needs ongoing external validation to stay accurate.
